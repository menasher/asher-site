On building this site — and the things I'm glad I cut.

I wanted a personal site that was fast to publish to and fast to load — in that order. A small confession about the analytics tag I almost installed.

I wanted a personal site that was fast to publish to and fast to load — in that order. The first part is what most personal sites get wrong, and the second is what most personal sites get fashionable about. I tried to swap the priorities.

The stack

Next.js 16 on the App Router, Tailwind v4 with CSS variables, shadcn-style primitives, and Velite for the content layer. Geist as the typeface — locally bundled because I learned the hard way that Google Fonts is not infrastructure.

That last one earned the "things I'm glad I cut" bullet. Three different build runs failed on font fetches before I gave up and switched to a local package. Lesson: anything you can't reach during your build is a runtime dependency in disguise.

Why MDX over a CMS

A CMS is overkill for a one-author site. MDX gives me version control on content, the ability to embed React in posts when I need it, and zero login flow when I want to publish. If I ever need a collaborator UI, I can layer a CMS on top later.

Done is a feature. Ship the small thing. Then ship the next small thing.

The trade-off is that I write in Cursor, not in a WYSIWYG. For me that's a win — every post starts as a draft in the same folder as the rest of the code, and gets the same kind of careful attention as a refactor.

"Anything you can't reach during your build is a runtime dependency in disguise."
